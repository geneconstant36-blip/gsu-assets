Frequently Asked Questions

What does the Area & Sheets Optimizer actually give me on the job site?

You build the surface the way you measured it — a rectangle, an L-shape, a trapezoid, a gable triangle, or a custom polygon, entered in honest feet-and-inches like 23'7″ by 16' — then add each window and door with its quantity, and the live blueprint redraws with dimension labels, dashed openings, and your net square footage printed at the center. From there the optimizer does the supply-house arithmetic: exact sheet counts for your chosen material and waste allowance, the smartest stock-length combination for any wall run, a visual cut diagram showing every seam, and a cost total. You measure once, and you order once.

How does the sheet count formula work?

Sheets = the net area times one-plus-your-waste-percentage, divided by the area of one sheet, rounded up — ceil(Net × (1 + Waste) ÷ SheetArea). A 4×8 sheet covers 32 square feet, a 4×10 covers 40, a 4×12 covers 48. The waste slider at 5, 10, 15, or 20 percent is not pessimism, it is experience: every cut around an outlet, every factory edge you trim, and every piece that cracks in the truck comes out of that allowance. Rounding up matters too — the supply house does not sell four-fifths of a sheet, and the tool never pretends it does.

How does the optimizer choose stock lengths for a wall run?

Check off the stock lengths your yard carries — 8, 10, and 12 foot — and for any run the tool lays out the realistic ways to make the length, each with its cut pieces, its waste in feet and percent, and its seam count, then pins a Least Waste badge on the winner. The tool tells you the trade-off straight, in its own words: least waste is often two eights, but that means more seams — the carpenter picks least waste plus fewest seams. The visual cut diagram draws every four-foot seam and length break so you can see exactly where the joints land before you commit a single cut.
