How does the rafter length math work?

Three formulas straight from Trades Algebra, chained together. Rise = Run × Pitch/12, so a 12-foot run at 6/12 rises exactly 6 feet. The slope factor is √((Pitch/12)² + 1) — 1.118 for a 6/12, climbing to 1.414 at a full 12/12 — and it is the single most useful number in roofing because it converts any flat measurement into its true length along the slope. The theoretical rafter is the hypotenuse √(Run² + Rise²), and the total rafter adds the overhang times the slope factor, because an 18-inch horizontal overhang is more than 18 inches of actual wood once it travels down the pitch. The tool runs the chain live and rounds to the sixteenth, exactly the way you would mark the tape.

What are the plumb cut, seat cut, and birdsmouth, and how does the tool handle them?

The plumb cut is the vertical cut where the rafter meets the ridge board, and its angle equals the pitch angle itself — 26.57° for a 6/12, a perfect 45° at 12/12. The seat cut is its complement, 90° minus the pitch angle, which is the horizontal cut that lets the rafter sit flat on the wall plate. Together the seat and a short plumb cut form the birdsmouth — the notch that seats the rafter on the plate — and the tool reminds you of the field rule that matters: a seat of about 3-1/2 inches to match the plate, and a notch depth no more than one-third of the rafter’s depth, because cutting deeper than that throws away the structural strength you paid for.

What is in the Real Cut List, and how are the quantities figured?

It is a genuine material takeoff, not a toy. Common rafters are counted per side as building length divided by on-center spacing, rounded up, plus one for the end — a 30-foot building at 16 inches on center needs 24 rafters per side, 48 total, each listed with its length to the sixteenth and both cut angles. The ridge board runs the building length. Sheathing area is the total rafter length times building length times two sides — just over 900 square feet on the default ranch — which the tool converts into 29 sheets of 4×8, about 9 roofing squares, 28 shingle bundles at three bundles per square, plus drip edge footage and underlayment rolls.
