Contractor’s Field Math

Four laminated-style cards for the jobsite: the geometry that keeps work square, the conversions that keep orders right, and the business math that keeps the doors open. Print it, tape it to the truck, keep it in the gang box. Math doesn’t make the job easier — it makes the mistakes smaller.

01

Know Your Dimensions + Slope + Circles

The Three Dimensions

1D — Length
One measurement. Trim, base, fascia.
2D — Area
Area = Length × Width
3D — Volume
Volume = L × W × H

Circles

Diameter
D = 2r
Circumference
C = π × D = 2πr  (π≈3.1416)
Area
A = πr²   shortcut: A ≈ 0.785 × D²

Slope & Right Triangles

Pythagoras
a² + b² = c²
Slope
Slope = Rise ÷ Run

3-4-5 Square Check

a²+b²=c² → 9+16=25
If legs are 3 & 4, the diagonal must be exactly 5 for square. Use any multiple: 6-8-10, 9-12-15.

Shapes, Conversions & Field Tricks

Common Shapes — Area & Volume

Square
A = s²
Rectangle
A = L × W
Triangle
A = (b × h) ÷ 2

A = (a+b)/2 × h

Temperature Math

C = (F − 32) × 5/9
F = C × 9/5 + 32
Example: 68°F = 20°C
Freezing: 32°F = 0°C   Comfort: 72°F ≈ 22°C
All decimals here use US standard. Divide inches by 12 on any calculator — no table needed in the field.
03

Common Trade Units & Flooring Math

Trade Coverage — How We Count

Paint: one gallon rolls ~350-400 sq ft per coat. Porous or textured drops to ~250-300.
Wallpaper: single roll ≈ 28 sq ft, double roll ≈ 56 sq ft. Match repeat eats 15-20%.
Tile: tiles needed = floor sq ft ÷ tile area. Add 10-15% waste, 18-20% for diagonal.
Carpet: sq yd = sq ft ÷ 9. Seamed broadloom needs extra for pattern and fill.
Concrete: cu ft = L × W × Thick(ft). Cu yd = cu ft ÷ 27. Order +5% for uneven grade.
Roofing: 1 square = 100 sq ft. Bundles per square vary (3-bund typical). Check ridge and starter.
Drywall / Sheathing: sheet 4×8 = 32 sq ft. Sheets = total area ÷ 32. Subtract only large openings >32 sf.

Board Feet — Lumber Logic

BF = (T" × W" × L') ÷ 12

Thickness in inches × width in inches × length in feet, divided by 12. One board foot = 144 cu in — a 1"×12"×12" block.

Ex: 2×6×10' → (2×6×10)/12 = 10 BF
Quarter sizes: 4/4=1", 5/4=1.25", 8/4=2"

Stair Math — Quick Layout

Number of risers = Total Rise ÷ Target Riser Height

Round to a whole count per comfort and code. Round risers, then: Exact Riser = Total Rise ÷ Count.

Run per tread = 10" to 11" (plus nosing). One less tread than risers if top is landing.

Quick Conversions On Site

Linear Feet: total length. Trim, base, fascia — just add runs.
Sq Ft → Sheets: Sheets = Area ÷ 32 (4×8). ÷ 48 for 4×12.
Board Foot: 1 BF = 144 cubic inches. Not linear, not square.
FIELD CHECK
Before you order: add waste, check bundle counts, confirm actual sheet size (some 4×9, 4×10). Measure twice, convert once.
04

Business Math for the Jobsite

Markup vs Margin — Don’t Confuse Them

Markup lives on cost. You add it:

Price = Cost × (1 + Markup%)

Margin lives on price. It’s what you keep:

Margin% = (Price − Cost) ÷ Price
Example: $100 cost + 30% markup = $130 price = 23.1% margin

To get 30% margin, you need 42.86% markup. Margin always looks smaller than markup for same dollars.

Percent Math You Actually Use

X% of N = N × (X÷100)
15% of 240 = 240×0.15 = 36
Add X%: N × (1 + X/100)
Remove X%: N × (1 − X/100)

What To Charge Per Hour

Break-Even $/hr = (Annual Overhead + Profit Goal) ÷ Billable Hrs

Overhead = rent, insurance, trucks, phones, non-billable time. Billable hrs = hours you can actually invoice (often 1,000-1,400/yr per person, not 2,080).

Ex: $85k overhead + $40k profit = $125k ÷ 1,200 hrs ≈ $104/hr

Frequently Asked Questions

Card 1 covers dimensions, slope, right triangles, and circles. Card 2 covers common shapes, inch-to-decimal-foot conversion, and field tricks. What is the 3-4-5 rule and why do contractors use it?
The 3-4-5 rule is a field application of the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²). Measure 3 feet along one edge and 4 feet along the other; if the corner is truly square, the diagonal between those marks must be exactly 5 feet, because 9 + 16 = 25. Any multiple works the same way — 6-8-10, 9-12-15 — which is useful for laying out larger walls, decks, and foundations without a framing square.
What is the difference between markup and margin?
Markup lives on cost: Price = Cost × (1 + Markup%). Margin lives on price: Margin% = (Price − Cost) ÷ Price. They are not the same number. A $100 cost with 30% markup produces a $130 price, but that job carries only a 23.1% margin. To actually keep a 30% margin you need a 42.86% markup. Confusing the two is one of the most common ways small contractors underprice their work.
How do I figure out what to charge per hour?
Break-Even $/hr = (Annual Overhead + Profit Goal) ÷ Billable Hours. Overhead includes rent, insurance, trucks, phones, and all non-billable time. Billable hours are the hours you can actually invoice — often only 1,000-1,400 per year per person, not the 2,080 hours in a full work year. Example: $85k overhead plus a $40k profit goal is $125k; divided by 1,200 billable hours, that’s roughly $104/hr just to break even and hit the goal.
How much waste should I add when ordering materials?
It depends on the material and the layout. Typical add-ons: hardwood straight 8-10%, hardwood diagonal or wide plank 12-15%, tile straight 12-15%, tile diagonal or large format 15-20%, carpet broadloom 5-7%, sheet vinyl 3-5%, roofing with cuts, valleys, and hips 7-12%, and lumber framing 10-15%. Pattern match, out-of-square walls, and small rooms all burn extra material — when in doubt, round up one bundle or box.
