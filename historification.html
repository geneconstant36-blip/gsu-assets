Franklin Connection

A Close Relationship That Shaped a Nation

C
B.F.
Canasatego × Franklin
Lancaster Treaty · 1744
"

It would be a very strange Thing, if six Nations of ignorant Savages should be capable of forming a Scheme for such an Union, and be able to execute it in such a Manner, as that it has subsisted Ages, and appears indissoluble; and yet that a like Union should be impracticable for ten or a dozen English Colonies.

— Benjamin Franklin, 1751, on the Haudenosaunee model
Printed treaties Council fire model Albany Plan 1754

Benjamin Franklin lived among, printed treaties for, and learned from Haudenosaunee leaders. In 1744 at Lancaster, Chief Canasatego advised the colonists to unite as the Five Nations had — a federal council that had already held for six centuries.

Ten years later, Franklin brought this model to the Albany Congress, drafting the Albany Plan of Union — the first proposal for colonial union. He printed the proceedings and kept the council fire as a model for American federalism. The idea did not start in Philadelphia. It was already burning in Onondaga.

Interpretive Layer · GSU Perspective

The Vocabulary of the American Story

Eight terms every citizen inherits — plain-language definition on top, GSU's interpretation underneath. Read a hard primary source with these in hand and the source will stop being hard.

Republic

A form of government where power resides in the people, who exercise it through elected representatives constrained by a written law. Not a direct democracy; not a monarchy; not an oligarchy.

The GSU Perspective:

The Founders chose the word republic, not democracy, deliberately. A democracy without limits is fifty-one people voting to take everything from the other forty-nine. A republic answers to a written law that even the majority cannot cross.

Confederation

A league of sovereign nations that keep their own governments but agree on shared matters through a common council. Each member remains free to act on its own; only enumerated concerns rise to the council.

The GSU Perspective:

The Haudenosaunee lived a working confederation for six centuries before Philadelphia. The Articles of Confederation failed by trying it without enough strength; the Constitution corrected that. Both drew from the same council fire.

Empire

A single state that rules many different peoples and territories beyond its own homeland, usually through force, tribute, or a mix of both, and often by installing viceroys or governors chosen from the ruling center.

The GSU Perspective:

Every empire promises order and exacts a price. GSU teaches you to read both sides of that bargain — the roads and the taxes, the peace and the boot, the market and the mine.

Constitution

The written framework establishing a government's structure, its enumerated powers, and its explicit limits. A citizen's contract with the state, ratified before the state was permitted to act.

The GSU Perspective:

The American Constitution is a document of enumerated powers — the government may do only what is listed. The Bill of Rights is not a gift; it is a fence. Most Americans have never been taught the difference, and every year without that teaching costs the citizen something.

Sovereignty

The ultimate authority to govern — to make binding law without appeal to a higher earthly power. In practice, sovereignty is located in whichever body has the last legitimate word.

The GSU Perspective:

The Founders located sovereignty in the people, not the government. The government is the agent; the citizen is the principal. Every generation that forgets this pays for the forgetting.

Federalism

The division of power between a national government and constituent states or nations. Some things belong to the whole; some things belong to the parts. The line between them is drawn in the constitution and defended by courts and citizens.

The GSU Perspective:

The Haudenosaunee lived federalism six centuries before Philadelphia named it. Their innovation — local autonomy plus collective security — is the American structure. Franklin studied it directly and brought its lessons to Albany in 1754.

Consent of the Governed

The principle that a government's legitimacy comes from the ongoing agreement of the people it rules — not from divine right, not from conquest, not from tradition alone. Withdraw consent long enough and the government's authority collapses.

The GSU Perspective:

The principle traces both to Locke and to the Haudenosaunee clan-mother selection of sachems — the earlier tradition operating a consent mechanism the philosopher would later theorize. Two rivers, one river mouth.
