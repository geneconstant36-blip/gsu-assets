How to Play

Three Small Discoveries

Every knob in this explorer teaches something real about the neighborhood. Try each one at least once and see what you notice.

1
Push Time Faster
Slide the time speed all the way up. Now watch how many times Mercury goes around the Sun in the time Neptune moves just a little. That is why one year on Neptune is 165 Earth years. Some planets have long childhoods.
2
Turn the Paths Off
Uncheck “Show Orbital Paths.” Now the planets look like they are floating on their own. This is how the neighborhood really looks in space \u2014 there are no drawn lines. The lines are our idea, not the sky’s.
3
Focus on Any Planet
Change the camera focus to Earth. Now Earth stays in the middle and the Sun moves around it. That is what our sky feels like from home. Ancient astronomers thought this was the truth for thousands of years \u2014 and it took real curiosity to figure out otherwise.

Straight Answers

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the planets the right size?
The sizes here are drawn for looking, not for scale. If Jupiter were drawn to real scale next to the Sun on your screen, Jupiter would be a dot and Earth would be almost invisible. What is honest here is the “order” \u2014 which planet is bigger than which, and where each one lives in the neighborhood.
Are the orbital speeds real?
Yes, in ratio. Mercury really does zip around the Sun much faster than Neptune \u2014 the way it looks on your screen matches the way it works in space. What we telescoped is total time: one full year of your explorer is not one real year. But the relative speeds are honest.
Where are the moons?
Earth\u2019s Moon and the biggest moons of Jupiter and Saturn are coming to the explorer in the next update. This first version shows just the eight planets and the Sun. Until the moons arrive, ask GENO about them \u2014 he can tell you every one.
Where is Pluto?
Pluto is a dwarf planet living out in the Kuiper Belt, past Neptune. This first version of the explorer stops at Neptune, but the icy edge \u2014 Pluto, the Kuiper Belt, and comets \u2014 is arriving in the next update. Chapter 7 of The Sun’s Family covers all of it in the meantime.
Can I use this in my homeschool lesson?
Absolutely. It is free forever, requires no login, no email, no download. Bookmark it. Project it. Have your child drive it while you read the corresponding chapter of The Sun’s Family out loud. That is how it was made to be used.
