Chapter One

The Eisenhower Warning Updated

Naming the Complex

In 1961, Eisenhower did something presidents rarely do on their way out the door: he tried to give the public a lens. Not a policy. Not a program. A way of seeing.

The words "military-industrial complex" were not a flourish. They were a schematic compressed into a phrase. Eisenhower was naming a relationship between institutions whose incentives aligned so cleanly that they did not need to meet in a back room. The system could run on autopilot. Defense contractors did not have to whisper to generals. Generals did not have to bribe legislators. Legislators did not have to invent enemies from whole cloth. Each part simply pursued its own continuation, its own budget, its own relevance, and the aggregate effect became a national gravitational field.

It is tempting, sixty-plus years later, to treat that phrase as history. To imagine that Eisenhower's warning belongs to the Cold War, the Pentagon, and the era of black-and-white television. But his deeper insight was not about tanks and missiles. It was about how modern America builds systems that manufacture their own necessity.

That is the pattern this book asks you to see.

The Fog-Industrial Complex is not a metaphor. It is a structural reality: an interconnected ecosystem of industries, institutions, and government agencies whose revenue, authority, and continued existence depend on the sustained production of human beings who cannot independently navigate a text-based civilization.

A text-based civilization is one in which rights, obligations, permissions, and penalties are delivered through documents. Laws are text. Contracts are text. Policies are text. Ballot measures are text. Medical instructions are text. Insurance exclusions are text. Debt instruments are text. Even the terms that govern our digital lives are text. In such a civilization, literacy is not enrichment. Literacy is sovereignty. And its absence is not merely a private hardship; it is a public supply chain.

Eisenhower's complex relied on the production of weapons and the presence of threats. This new complex relies on the production of fog and the presence of incapacity. In the military-industrial complex, the raw materials are steel, research, and fear. In the Fog-Industrial Complex, the raw material is human confusion.

The shift from one complex to the other does not require you to believe that America "changed" into something sinister. It requires you to recognize that large systems evolve toward stable revenue. When a society tolerates widespread illiteracy and surrounds it with bureaucratic and legal language too dense for ordinary people to parse, it accidentally creates a market. Then it does what markets do: it professionalizes the gap.

We like to speak about "access" as if access were the goal. But access to what, exactly? Access to services that exist because the system never built capacity in the first place? Access to intermediaries who stand between the citizen and the document? Access to someone who can translate what should never have been written in code?

The central move of the Fog-Industrial Complex is not to deny people help. The central move is to make help permanently necessary.

This is where the parallel to Eisenhower becomes precise. The complex is not a single institution. It is not a villain. It is an incentive structure that recruits participants. The individual nodes do not have to coordinate. Nobody has to conspire. The incentive structure does the conspiring.

Consider how the military-industrial complex was stabilized: budgets that renewed annually; career pathways that depended on continuation; political fear that rewarded escalation; technical language that turned moral questions into engineering questions; classified systems that limited oversight. Now translate that architecture into civilian life.

In the Fog-Industrial Complex, the factory at the front end is education. That claim is not moralistic; it is logistical. If a nation produces graduates who cannot reliably decode print, cannot track a multi-step argument, cannot distinguish a claim from evidence, cannot read a lease clause and predict its real-world consequence, then that nation has mass-produced the customer base for every fog intermediary downstream.

And once those downstream industries mature, they begin to function like defense contractors in Eisenhower's era. They develop professional associations. They develop standards. They develop "best practices." They develop lobbying arms and marketing campaigns and credential requirements. They become respectable. They become normal. They become part of the background of American life. They do not see themselves as profiting from failure. They see themselves as responding to need. That is how a complex survives moral scrutiny: it recasts dependency as compassion.

This is why the Fog-Industrial Complex is so difficult to confront. It hides inside good intentions. It dresses itself in the language of assistance. It uses the vocabulary of equity, support, access, services, outreach, navigation, empowerment. Those words are not inherently dishonest. The dishonesty is structural: the system expands the need for "empowerment" while quietly protecting the conditions that keep people disempowered.

In my previous work, Educated into Ignorance, I named the wound. Balanced Literacy taught a generation to guess instead of read. Diplomas were issued as credentials for skills that were never built. Millions of adults carry Educational Betrayal Syndrome, the disorienting trauma of discovering that the system that certified them did not equip them. That betrayal is not merely personal; it is economic. It is the entry ticket into a lifetime of paid translation.

This book maps what happens next.

Once the education system has produced an adult who cannot confidently read and reason through the documents that govern modern life, the adult does not simply suffer in isolation. The adult becomes a unit of predictable demand.

They need benefits navigators because public assistance applications are written in fog. They need tax preparation services because tax forms and instructions have been allowed to become a private language. They need legal aid because leases, employment agreements, and consumer contracts are designed with plausible deniability, written to be defensible rather than understandable. They need healthcare intermediaries because consent forms and discharge instructions prioritize liability shields over patient comprehension. They need for-profit colleges and certificate programs because the first credential did not deliver competence, and the culture has taught them that the solution to a skills deficit is another piece of paper. They need remediation programs to fix what K-12 was supposed to build, and those programs are often sold by the same publishers who sold the methods that failed.

In the military-industrial complex, weapons manufacturing does not end war, because war sustains demand. In the Fog-Industrial Complex, fog manufacturing does not end confusion, because confusion sustains demand. And fog is not only produced by poorly written documents. Fog is also produced by policy choices, legal conventions, institutional habits, and credential systems that measure inputs rather than outputs.

It is crucial here to separate this argument from conspiracy theory, because the Fog-Industrial Complex will always be defended with the same lazy counterattack: "Are you saying all these people got together to make Americans illiterate?" No. That question is a smokescreen designed to protect incentives by forcing critics to argue about secret meetings.

Complexes do not require secret meetings. They require predictable self-interest and a refusal to track downstream consequences. They require the kind of compartmentalization that modern professional life encourages.

The curriculum developer says, "I'm just following the framework." The administrator says, "We're improving graduation rates." The testing company says, "We measure standards." The ed-tech vendor says, "We provide tools." The benefits agency says, "We must prevent fraud." The insurer says, "We must comply with regulations." The hospital says, "We must document consent." The lender says, "We disclose as required." The politician says, "The bill is complex because the issue is complex."

Each statement can be sincere. Each statement can be technically defensible. But the aggregate effect is a society in which the citizen is constantly asked to sign, consent, agree, comply, renew, enroll, and certify without comprehension. The citizen becomes a subject in a paperwork kingdom. And in that kingdom, the intermediaries become the nobility: the people who can read, interpret, file, negotiate, and translate. A literate person can move directly. A semi-literate person must move through handlers.

This is what I mean when I say fog is not a communication failure. Fog is a product with market value.

If you want to see it as a product, ask the simple question every business analyst asks: who benefits when comprehension fails?

When a customer does not understand a contract, disputes decrease, cancellations decrease, renewals increase, and fees become easier to extract. When a patient does not understand discharge instructions, follow-up calls and readmissions rise. When a benefits applicant cannot understand eligibility rules, fewer benefits are granted and administrative workloads can be justified through "case complexity." When a voter cannot parse a ballot measure, the campaign with the bigger marketing budget wins. When a student cannot read proficiently but still graduates, the system avoids accountability in the short term and sells "support services" in the long term.

The market value of fog lies in asymmetry. Fog creates a gap between the institution that writes the text and the person forced to live under it. That gap is power. Power can be converted into money, compliance, and control.

This is why the Fog-Industrial Complex is more pervasive than Eisenhower's complex, even if it is less dramatic. A tank is visible. A fighter jet is visible. A defense contract may be opaque, but the existence of the military is obvious. Fog, by contrast, is experienced as normal life. You are supposed to feel stupid. You are supposed to feel overwhelmed. You are supposed to assume that competent adults hire experts for this. You are supposed to believe that if you cannot understand the language, the problem is you.

That belief is the system's greatest asset. It turns institutional design into personal shame. It keeps people from demanding what they should demand: clarity.

If a nation's laws, medical instructions, financial obligations, and public services are written in a language that ordinary citizens cannot read, then that nation has not merely failed at education. It has constructed an economy around the failure. And once an economy is built, it will defend itself the way all economies defend themselves: by normalizing its assumptions, credentialing its gatekeepers, and framing critique as ignorance.

The purpose of naming this complex is not to villainize individuals inside it. Most people working in education, healthcare, government, and finance are not cartoon antagonists. They are human beings doing jobs inside systems whose incentives they did not design. But a complex cannot be dismantled by applauding intentions. It can only be dismantled by changing incentives and restoring sovereignty at the individual level.

That is why the clearing begins with a phrase. Eisenhower gave the nation a phrase that made a machine visible. This book offers another phrase for a different machine, one that operates not in distant theaters of war but in your mailbox, your inbox, your child's backpack, your HR portal, your lease renewal, and the documents you sign when you are scared and tired in a hospital waiting room.

The military-industrial complex profited from perpetual readiness. The Fog-Industrial Complex profits from perpetual dependence. Different products, same architecture.

And just as Eisenhower's warning asked citizens to become vigilant about the relationship between industry and the state, this warning asks citizens to become vigilant about the relationship between literacy and freedom. Because in a text-based civilization, the person who cannot read is not merely disadvantaged.

They are governable. They are billable. They are manageable.
They are exploitable.

The rest of this chapter will map the feedback loop that sustains that exploitation and explain why, in a nation that prides itself on freedom, the ability to read your own life may be the most radical form of independence left.

The Feedback Loop

The moment you say "complex," many readers hear "conspiracy." They picture a conference room, a secret agenda, a shared document, a handshake between villains. That reflex is understandable, because conspiracy is the only mental model most people have for coordinated harm. But it is also the reflex that protects modern systems from scrutiny. If the only way a system can be guilty is if it is intentional, then any machine built out of incentives can run forever.

Eisenhower was warning the public about exactly that kind of machine. He was describing a system that produced outcomes without needing a mastermind. A complex is what happens when continuation becomes the shared goal of many separate institutions, each of which can defend its actions as reasonable in isolation. Nobody has to plot. Everybody only has to keep their job.

The Fog-Industrial Complex operates by the same logic. The education system does not need to meet with the payday lender. The hospital does not need to coordinate with the tax preparation franchise. The benefits agency does not need to call the for-profit college. They are connected by something more reliable than a phone call: the output of one becomes the input of the next.

That is the feedback loop. It is not mysterious. It is mechanical.

Start at the front end: a child enters school. If the system teaches that child to read through methods that emphasize guessing, cueing, and "meaning-making" without mastery of decoding, a predictable portion of students will emerge as partial readers: people who can perform literacy in familiar contexts but cannot reliably decode new words, sustain attention through dense paragraphs, or follow a multi-step argument without losing the thread. In Educated into Ignorance, I called this the Guessing Generation. In this book, I want you to see the next step: what happens when that guessing reader becomes an adult in a text-based civilization.

The adult is then asked to do what the modern state and modern economy require of citizens: interpret documents, comply with procedures, consent to obligations, and advocate for themselves inside institutions that speak in specialized language. The adult cannot do this independently, not consistently, not under stress, and not when the text has been engineered for defensibility rather than clarity. So the adult seeks help. Help becomes a service. Services become industries.

Industries, once established, develop their own continuation instincts: budgets, headcounts, certification pathways, "best practices," conferences, software platforms, and political relationships. These institutions then become stakeholders in the conditions that create demand. Most never articulate it as "we need people to stay confused." They articulate it as "we are addressing a persistent need." That is how incentives talk when they want to sound moral.

Now close the loop: when enough industries exist downstream that profit from incomprehension, the system becomes resistant to upstream repair. Not necessarily through sabotage. Resistance is usually passive. It looks like delay, dilution, and endless pilot programs. It looks like "stakeholder input." It looks like committees and task forces that study what we already know. It looks like "implementation challenges." It looks like professional turf wars. It looks like "the science isn't settled" long after it is settled. It looks like a thousand small reasons nothing fundamental changes.

That is how incentives replace conspiracy. Conspiracy requires coordination. Incentives require only self-preservation.

To make this concrete, imagine the loop as a supply chain with five stations.

Station one is the literacy factory. This is K-12, teacher training programs, curriculum publishers, and the ideological commitments that decide what counts as "reading instruction." In a functioning system, the quality-control metric would be: can the student decode accurately, fluently, and independently? Can they read unfamiliar text and extract meaning without guessing? But the system often chooses easier metrics: graduation rates, seat time, credit accumulation, "engagement," and whether students can produce the appearance of comprehension during guided instruction. That shift matters because appearances can be managed. Competence cannot.

Station two is the fog environment. This is the surrounding world of documents: leases, insurance policies, medical consent forms, HR handbooks, school communications, benefits applications, loan agreements, and digital terms. The fog environment has its own incentives. Documents are often written to reduce liability, preserve discretion, discourage challenges, and create plausible deniability. Complexity becomes a shield. A clause that a tenth grader can't parse is rarely an accident; it is often a legal strategy. Institutions can say, "We disclosed it," because it exists on paper. The paper becomes the moral alibi.

Station three is the intermediary market. When ordinary people cannot navigate station two, a new category of worker expands: navigators, advocates, specialists, preparers, consultants, case managers, eligibility workers, compliance officers, and the entire universe of "let me help you with that" professionals. Many of these people are sincere. Many are doing necessary work. The point is not to insult them; it is to ask why their jobs are necessary at all. A society that requires millions of intermediaries to help adults interpret routine documents is not a sophisticated society. It is a society operating with a comprehension deficit so large it has been professionalized.

Station four is monetization. Once the intermediary market exists, it becomes profitable to keep it busy. Confusion turns into revenue in multiple ways. Some are direct, like fees for tax preparation or legal services. Others are indirect, like renewals that continue because customers cannot compare terms, or medical errors that increase downstream spending, or benefits that go unclaimed because the application reads like a foreign language. Fog makes it easier to extract money, but it also makes it easier to extract compliance. The person who cannot argue with the document must submit to it.

Station five is normalization. The final station is psychological and cultural. People are trained to accept fog as adulthood. "Nobody understands insurance." "Taxes are complicated." "You need a professional." "That's just legal language." "This is above my pay grade." These phrases function like sedation. They convert structural failure into personal resignation. When resignation becomes normal, the loop becomes stable.

Notice what is missing from this loop: a villain. The loop runs because each station benefits from its role. It runs because the costs are scattered across millions of individuals, while the profits and institutional advantages are concentrated. It runs because the person harmed is also the person least equipped to explain how they were harmed.

That last point is crucial. In a text-based civilization, the ability to describe the mechanism is itself a form of power. A citizen who cannot read the policy cannot challenge the policy. A worker who cannot parse the handbook cannot contest the handbook. A patient who cannot interpret a discharge instruction cannot advocate for an alternative. A borrower who cannot evaluate APR cannot compare offers. Fog does not only take money. Fog takes the language of resistance.

This is why the Complex can hide inside good intentions. Consider the standard defense offered by each node.

The school says, "We're doing our best with limited resources." The curriculum vendor says, "We align to the standards." The teacher preparation program says, "Reading is complex; there is no single method." The district says, "We need to focus on the whole child." The testing company says, "We provide data." The remediation provider says, "We support struggling students." The college says, "We provide opportunity." The lender says, "We disclose as required." The agency says, "We must prevent fraud." The hospital says, "We must document consent." The lawyer says, "This language is necessary."

Each statement contains a sliver of truth. That is what makes the machine durable. A complex is not built out of lies; it is built out of partial truths that never have to confront their cumulative effect.

And because the cumulative effect is never assigned to any single actor, accountability dissolves. The student who graduates unable to read proficiently is told it is their effort, their attention span, their home environment, their motivation. The adult who cannot navigate forms is told the system is complex, and complex things require experts. The citizen who fails to claim a benefit is told they should have asked for help. The borrower trapped in a predatory loan is told they signed the contract. The patient harmed by misunderstanding is told they consented.

This is consent without comprehension, but it is also blame without design. The institution designs the fog; the individual receives the shame.

Now add one more stabilizer to the feedback loop: the credential. Credentials are supposed to signal competence. In the Fog-Industrial Complex, credentials often signal participation. The diploma tells employers and the public, "This person is ready." When that signal becomes unreliable, society does not abandon the credential. It piles on more credentials. More tests. More certificates. More "continuing education." More hoops. The credential inflation creates an additional market for training and verification, which further entrenches the idea that competence is something you purchase in installments rather than build as a baseline human capacity.

This is why I call it credential laundering. The institution converts its own failure to teach into a piece of paper that implies the student possesses what the institution did not deliver. The paper protects the institution, not the student. The student then spends years paying to acquire the competence the paper pretended to certify.

If you step back and look at the loop from above, you can see why the "conspiracy" accusation is not just wrong, but convenient. If critics can be dismissed as paranoid, nobody has to examine the architecture. But the architecture is visible if you know what to look for: an upstream failure that produces downstream demand, and downstream industries whose growth depends on the upstream failure continuing.

This is the same schematic Eisenhower tried to give the nation, translated into civilian life. The products are different, but the logic is identical: incentives create self-reinforcing systems that manufacture their own necessity. The difference is that the military-industrial complex is loud and dramatic, while the Fog-Industrial Complex is quiet and intimate. It arrives in envelopes. It speaks in polite fonts. It waits for you to click "I agree."

The purpose of mapping the feedback loop is not to make you cynical. It is to make you precise. Cynicism says, "Everything is corrupt." Precision says, "Here is the mechanism, and here is where it can be interrupted."

Because once you can see the loop, you can ask the only question that matters in a complex: what would happen if the raw material supply changed?

In the military-industrial complex, the raw material is the perception of threat. In the Fog-Industrial Complex, the raw material is human incapacity in the face of text. Change that, and the system begins to starve. Not through outrage. Through withdrawal.

That withdrawal begins with the simplest and most radical shift available to a citizen in a text-based civilization: refusing to be managed through language you cannot verify. The rest of this chapter will sharpen that lens further by showing how fog becomes a product with market value, and why, once you understand that, you stop mistaking incomprehension for your personal failure and start recognizing it as someone else's business model.

Fog as a Product with Market Value

If you want to understand why fog persists, stop treating it like a mistake.

In most people's minds, incomprehensible language belongs in the same category as potholes and bad customer service: evidence that someone somewhere is incompetent, rushed, or indifferent. But in complex systems, recurring "mistakes" deserve a harder question. What if the confusion is not an accident of poor writing, but a feature of the business model? What if the unreadable document is not the failure, but the product?

Fog has market value because comprehension changes the power relationship.

When two parties understand the same text, they are negotiating. When one party writes the text and the other cannot parse it, the interaction is no longer a negotiation; it is an extraction. The institution becomes the adult in the room, not because it is morally superior, but because it controls the language. This is the central advantage that fog sells: asymmetry. The moment you see fog as asymmetry, you can see why it is profitable across sectors that appear unrelated.

The school that sends home a report card full of euphemisms and acronyms is not only communicating poorly. It is managing perception and liability. The hospital that hands you a consent form written at a graduate reading level is not only being "technical." It is building a legal shield. The lender that buries the true cost of borrowing inside a forest of disclosures is not merely following tradition. It is protecting a revenue stream that depends on the borrower's inability to compare. The government agency that publishes a benefits application in a dialect of bureaucratic English is not merely being formal. It is controlling access, preserving discretion, and, often, quietly reducing the number of successful applicants without ever having to deny them outright.

Fog does not have to be perfect to be profitable. It only has to be good enough that most people will not challenge it.

This is why "we disclosed it" has become the moral alibi of modern institutions. Disclosure is treated as a ritual. Put the words on paper. Place them in a portal. Have the consumer sign the line or click the box. The institution then claims consent, and the burden shifts to the individual: you should have read it, you should have understood it, you should have asked questions.

But in a text-based civilization, the crucial question is not whether information exists. The crucial question is whether an ordinary citizen can reasonably extract meaning from it.

When a system produces millions of adults who cannot reliably decode print under pressure, who read slowly, who cannot hold a long clause in working memory long enough to reach the end of the sentence, the institution receives a predictable gift: fewer disputes. Fewer appeals. Fewer complaints that cite the exact paragraph and the exact contradiction. Fewer citizens who can take the document home, read it twice, and return as a counterparty instead of a subject.

That gift can be turned into money in at least five reliable ways.

First, fog reduces friction in sales. The easier it is to enroll someone without full comprehension, the larger the customer base. Think about the way modern agreements are structured: the most consequential terms are placed where attention is lowest. At the end of a checkout flow. On page fourteen. Behind a hyperlink. After the words "for more information." The enrollment experience is streamlined; the meaning is not. Frictionless enrollment is not a technological achievement alone. It is also a literacy exploit. The less you understand, the easier you are to enroll.

Second, fog increases fee capture. Fees flourish in the shadows. Late fees, overdraft fees, rate resets, penalties, exclusions, "processing" charges, "administrative" charges, "out-of-network" charges, and the whole modern taxonomy of money that appears after you thought you were done paying. A literate consumer reads the triggers. A semi-literate consumer learns about them by getting hit. Fog converts contracts into traps that look, from the institution's perspective, like "consumer choice."

Third, fog reduces claims and challenges. Insurance is a simple example. If exclusions are written in language the customer cannot translate into real-world scenarios, many valid claims will never be filed, and many denied claims will never be appealed. The customer will assume the denial is final because the explanation is written as if it were inevitable. The institution can truthfully say, "The policyholder was notified." That sentence is technically accurate and functionally meaningless, which is why it is so useful.

Fourth, fog preserves institutional discretion. Bureaucracies thrive on discretionary power because discretion is control. If a form is clear, a citizen can tell whether they qualify. If the criteria are foggy, the citizen must submit themselves to the agency's interpretation. Now the agency is not merely administering a rule; it is performing judgment. That judgment can be consistent or inconsistent, compassionate or harsh, but either way it expands the agency's authority. Fog makes discretion possible. Clarity makes discretion expensive, because clarity creates enforceable expectations.

Fifth, fog shifts the cost of error onto the individual. When instructions are unclear, the user will make mistakes. The system then penalizes the mistake as if it were a moral failing. Missed deadlines, incomplete forms, incorrect documentation, misunderstood dosage instructions, wrong coverage assumptions. The institution can say, "You didn't follow the procedure." Fog creates the procedure, then punishes the person who cannot see it.

Each of these mechanisms has an economic signature. They increase revenue, reduce cost, and decrease accountability.

Now connect this back to the factory at the front end: education. In the previous section, we mapped the feedback loop and named the Guessing Generation produced by Balanced Literacy, the population taught to use context clues and prediction where decoding skill should have been. Here is the downstream consequence that matters for this subchapter: a guessing reader is uniquely vulnerable to fog.

A guessing reader is often socially functional. They can navigate familiar texts. They can infer meaning in conversation. They can skim. They can recognize some words by sight. But fog is not written to be skimmable. Fog is written to defeat skimming. It is written in nested clauses, defined terms, cross-references, exceptions to exceptions, and paragraphs that require you to hold multiple conditions in your mind simultaneously. Fog is engineered to overload the exact cognitive muscles that weak readers lack.

This is why the Fog-Industrial Complex does not rely on total illiteracy. It relies on partial literacy, the kind that produces compliance without comprehension. Total illiteracy is visible; partial illiteracy is deniable. It produces adults who can be told, sincerely, "You should have read the agreement," and who will blame themselves because they can read enough to feel the shame of not reading well.

That shame is not incidental. It is a stabilizer.

Shame keeps people from asking questions in public. Shame keeps them from requesting plain-language versions. Shame keeps them from bringing a friend to read alongside them. Shame keeps them from disputing fees or appealing denials. Shame also keeps them buying intermediaries quietly, like someone purchasing an inhaler instead of addressing the smoke in the building.

And once intermediaries exist, fog becomes even more valuable, because fog creates dependence not only on the institution, but on the translator class. This is where a harsh but clarifying truth emerges: fog is not merely a text. Fog is a labor market.

Every time a document becomes unreadable, a new category of paid guide becomes normal. Tax preparers. Benefits navigators. Patient advocates. HR consultants. Compliance officers. Enrollment specialists. Financial advisors for people who do not have enough money to need "advice," only enough confusion to be sold an expensive product. Some of these roles are noble in intent. Some are predatory. Most are simply inevitable in a society that refuses to treat literacy as infrastructure.

But notice what happens to the political conversation once a labor market forms around fog. Simplification begins to look like job destruction. Clarity becomes "unrealistic" because "the world is complex," and because, quietly, millions of paychecks now depend on the complexity remaining unsolved. The navigator is not the villain. The villain is the normalization of the need for navigators.

This is also why regulation so often fails to fix the problem. Regulation tends to add text. It creates disclosures, notices, acknowledgments, compliance statements, and procedural steps. Each step may be justified. Each may even be well-intentioned. But the cumulative effect is often more fog, not less. The institution becomes able to say, "We complied," while the citizen becomes even more buried under language.

Fog is not threatened by more rules.
Fog is threatened by readable rules.

That distinction matters because it reveals the real reform battlefield. The fight is not only about what policies exist. The fight is about whether the governed can read the policies without hiring a priesthood.

At this point, a reader may object: "Surely nobody sits down and says, 'Let's make this incomprehensible so we can profit.'" Sometimes they do, but they do not need to. This is where the continuity with Eisenhower's lens becomes exact again. The complex does not require malice. It requires incentives.

A lawyer drafts to defend against litigation. A compliance department drafts to satisfy auditors. A bureaucrat drafts to prevent fraud and preserve discretion. A marketer drafts to reduce drop-off at checkout. A curriculum vendor drafts to align with standards language and avoid political landmines. Each actor is rewarded for risk reduction and institutional protection, not for citizen comprehension. Clarity, in many organizations, is not a promotion pathway. Defensibility is.

So fog accumulates the way sediment accumulates in a riverbed. Not because the river hates the people downstream, but because each upstream action deposits a little more material, and nobody is paid to dredge it.

The result is a civilization where the ability to read is not merely academic. It is the difference between being a customer and being a target.

In a healthy society, literacy shrinks the intermediary market because people can handle routine life directly. In the Fog-Industrial Complex, literacy threatens revenue. That is the diagnostic test. When clarity threatens a business model, the business model is not built around serving the customer; it is built around managing the customer.

This is why the phrase "fog is a product with market value" is not rhetorical. It is literal. Fog is sold in the form of documents that people cannot challenge. Fog is sold as complexity that people must pay to navigate. Fog is sold as the normal background of adulthood. And the price is paid in fees, in time, in missed opportunities, in preventable mistakes, in dependency, and in the quiet internalization of the belief that modern life is designed for someone smarter than you.

But that belief is the final fog, the one inside the mind.

The clearing starts when you recognize that confusion is often profitable to someone else. Not because everyone is evil, but because systems reward outcomes, not intentions. Once you recognize that, the shame begins to lift, and a different posture becomes possible. You stop assuming the document is beyond you. You start assuming the document was written to control you.

And that shift in assumption is the first move from subject to citizen, from managed to sovereign.

The Withdrawal Because the moment a population learns to treat clarity as a requirement, not a luxury, fog begins to lose its market value.
