Free · Plain-Language Glossary

GSU Reading Dictionary

The words teachers and researchers use when they talk about reading — explained in plain language, free, for anyone. Each entry tells you what the term means and why it matters. This is the vocabulary of the Science of Reading, the field that explains how the human brain actually learns to read.

PhonemeThe smallest unit of sound in spoken language. The word "cat" has three: /k/ /a/ /t/.Why it matters: Reading begins in the ear, not the eye — children must hear these sounds before letters mean anything.
Phonemic awarenessThe ability to hear, identify, and play with the individual sounds in spoken words — with no letters in front of you.Why it matters: It is the single strongest early predictor of who will learn to read easily.
Phonological awarenessThe broader awareness of the sound structure of language — rhymes, syllables, and word parts — with phonemic awareness as its finest grain.
GraphemeA letter or group of letters that stands for one sound. In "ship," the letters "sh" are one grapheme for the sound /sh/.
PhonicsInstruction that teaches the link between graphemes (letters) and phonemes (sounds) so a reader can work out words.Why it matters: It hands a child the code instead of asking them to memorize thousands of words by sight.
DecodingTurning printed words into speech by applying letter-sound knowledge — "sounding it out."
EncodingThe reverse of decoding: turning the sounds you hear into written letters. This is spelling.
BlendingPushing separate sounds together to read a word: /m/ /a/ /p/ becomes "map."
SegmentingBreaking a spoken word back into its separate sounds. This is the key move behind spelling.
Orthographic mappingThe brain's process of storing a written word for instant, effortless recall by bonding its sounds, letters, and meaning together.Why it matters: This is how words truly become "sight words" — mapped, not memorized as pictures.
Sight wordsWords a reader recognizes instantly, without sounding out. With enough practice, nearly every word a person knows becomes a sight word.Why it matters: A common myth says these are memorized whole; in truth they are built by orthographic mapping.
MorphemeThe smallest unit of meaning in a word. "Unhelpful" contains three: un- + help + -ful.
MorphologyThe study of morphemes — how prefixes, roots, and suffixes build and change words.Why it matters: It unlocks the spelling and meaning of long words that can't be sounded out letter by letter.
FluencyReading with accuracy, a comfortable pace, and natural expression.Why it matters: It is the bridge between decoding words and understanding them — a struggling decoder has no attention left for meaning.
ProsodyThe rhythm, stress, and rise-and-fall of reading aloud — reading that "sounds like talking," not robotic.
VocabularyThe store of words a person knows and uses.Why it matters: You cannot understand a sentence built from words you don't know.
ComprehensionThe whole point of reading: building meaning from the text.
Background knowledgeWhat a reader already knows about a topic before they start.Why it matters: It quietly does much of the heavy lifting in understanding — the more you know, the more you grasp.
SyllableA unit of pronunciation built around one vowel sound. Chunking long words into syllables makes them readable.
The Simple View of ReadingA research framework: Reading Comprehension = Decoding x Language Comprehension.Why it matters: Both factors are required. Strong decoding with weak language — or the reverse — still breaks down into poor reading.
