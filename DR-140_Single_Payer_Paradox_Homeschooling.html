The Single-Payer Paradox

Homeschooling and the Fall of the Public School Monopoly

An analytical report on the historical fracture of the American public school monopoly, the 2025 wave of universal school choice legislation, and the funding paradox that homeschool families should understand before accepting state money.

📊 ~4700-word analysis 📚 39 cited sources ⏱ ~17 min read

A 2025 national survey found that 75 percent of US parents support universal private school choice — the highest level recorded in any measurement of American educational opinion. Within eighteen months, five states enacted universal school-choice legislation. The public-school monopoly that took two centuries to build is fracturing in real time.

But the same mechanism that delivers school choice — public money flowing to private schools and homeschool families — carries the seed of the next monopoly. This is the Single-Payer Paradox, and homeschool families should understand it before they accept the first state check.

This Deep Research piece traces the historical arc of the American public-school monopoly from the 1647 Massachusetts Bay Decree through the 2025 legislative wave, names the intellectual figures who built and dismantled the consolidation, maps the structural mechanics of how state-funded school choice can quietly recapture the independence it claims to protect, and closes with GSU's structural position on the question of why a free educational foundation that accepts no government money is more important after universal school choice arrives, not less. The analysis is built on thirty-nine cited sources spanning Hoover, Cato, Brookings, Reason, UCLA Law Review, the JHU Institute for Education Policy, AASA, Race Forward, and First Things — a cross-ideological citation balance assembled deliberately to resist partisan dismissal of the conclusions.

The Historical Genesis of the Public School Monopoly

The development of structured, state-controlled schooling in the West can be traced to two transformative historical events: the invention of the Gutenberg printing press in 1455 and the Protestant Reformation in 1517. By mass-producing texts, the printing press broke the learning monopoly held by medieval monasteries, while the theological tenets of John Calvin established reading as a direct spiritual requirement, laying the groundwork for universal literacy. In the American colonies, these Calvinist roots led to early educational mandates, notably the 1647 decree of the General Court of the Massachusetts Bay Colony. This law required towns of fifty families to support an elementary school and those of one hundred families to establish a Latin grammar school, ensuring children could read the Bible and understand their religion.

Following the American Revolution, educational policy remained localized and fragmented. In 1779, Thomas Jefferson proposed a two-track system in Virginia, dividing students into the "laboring and the learned" and aiming to "rake a few geniuses from the rubbish". National infrastructure support began with the Continental Congress's Land Ordinance of 1785, which reserved land in each township to fund local public schools, eventually laying the foundation for land-grant universities. In 1790, the Pennsylvania state constitution called for free education, but only for impoverished children, expecting wealthy families to pay for private instruction. In urban centers, philanthropists and businessmen established the New York Public School Society in 1805, adopting the Lancasterian monitorial system. This model utilized a single schoolmaster to oversee hundreds of children in a single room, relying on older pupils to pass rote lessons down to younger ones—a structure designed to cultivate the discipline, punctuality, and obedience demanded by factory owners.

A turning point occurred in Boston in 1818, revealing the interventionist nature of early public school advocates. A municipal survey showed that while eight public schools served 2,360 pupils, a thriving private market of 150 dame and grammar schools successfully educated over 4,000 children. Only a negligible fraction of children lacked access to instruction. Despite this widespread literacy, municipal merchants and Whig reformers petitioned for compulsory, tax-supported primary schooling to curb perceived juvenile delinquency and displace private institutions. Wage earners initially opposed the initiative due to the accompanying tax burdens, but the state embarked on a campaign that ultimately forced children into public systems designed to disseminate Unitarian philosophy.

By the 1830s, the Common School Movement emerged as a unified national campaign. Led by Horace Mann in Massachusetts and Henry Barnard in Connecticut, this reform coalition sought to replace the decentralized marketplace with state-controlled, non-sectarian common schools. Mann famously argued in his 1848 annual report that universal education would serve as the "great equalizer" and the "balance wheel of the social machinery," preventing poverty rather than merely disarming the poor. Whig politicians like Governors DeWitt Clinton of New York and Edward Everett of Massachusetts, alongside Pennsylvania legislator Thaddeus Stevens, supported these reforms, framing them as essential internal improvements akin to highways. In the South, Calvin Wiley pursued similar standardization as North Carolina’s first superintendent of common schools, even as Southern legislatures enacted draconian bans on teaching enslaved populations to read.

Mann's reforms were heavily backed by Northern industrialists, such as Edmund Dwight, who personally supplemented Mann's salary to ensure the development of a cooperative, structured workforce. However, the common schools also acted as tools of social control and Protestant homogenization. The mandatory use of the King James Bible provoked fierce resistance from Irish Catholic immigrants, leading to struggles for neighborhood control in New York City and the Philadelphia Bible Riots of 1844. Courts consistently defended the Protestant curriculum, culminating in state supreme court rulings like Maine's 1854 decision and subsequent expulsions of Catholic students in Boston and New York. Furthermore, Mann’s legacy was complicated by his advocacy of oralism for deaf children, which operated on the premise that intellect required spoken language, causing long-term damage to deaf education.

Bureaucratic Consolidation and the Institutional Critique

Following the Civil War, the public school monopoly underwent systematic administrative consolidation. During the Progressive Era, reformers sought to eliminate decentralized, ward-based schooling and establish professionalized governance. They decided that a single administrative body—the school district—should own and operate all public schools within a designated geographic area. This model facilitated the state's modern constitutional obligations to provide education, but it also locked public funding into a single-provider system.

The structural consequences of this geographic monopoly became a central focus of educational sociology and economics. Robert B. Everhart’s landmark 1982 volume, The Public School Monopoly: A Critical Analysis of Education and the State in American Society, brought together scholars to examine the coercive relationship between the state and schooling. Within this text, Stephen Arons and Charles Lawrence III provided a constitutional critique, arguing that public schools manipulate student consciousness. They asserted that because public schools must choose a dominant set of values, they inevitably reproduce majoritarian interests, leaving little room for genuine pluralism or dissent. This "socialization by persuasion" is supported by state-enforced compulsory attendance and standardized curricula.

Additionally, economists like E.G. West utilized bureaucratic public choice theory to show that school districts and the federal Department of Education—established as a cabinet-level agency under President Jimmy Carter in 1979—operate as self-maximizing bureaucracies. West argued that these institutions insulate themselves from parental influence, leading to high administrative overhead, inefficient spending, and parental apathy. Empirical analyses of the 1972–1981 period showed that the sharpest declines in student test scores occurred in states where legislatures were most responsive to teachers' unions, such as the American Federation of Teachers (AFT).

This institutional critique was reinforced by researchers who connected the public monopoly to broader social crises. Sol Stern's Breaking Free highlighted how public school unions actively resist structural reforms and parental choices, arguing that California's escalating educational expenditures failed to yield higher academic performance. Stern and other researchers noted that independent Catholic schools frequently educated minority and low-income students more effectively and at half the per capita cost of their public counterparts. Similarly, in The School-to-Prison Pipeline (2024), Peter Thalheim argued that the public school monopoly and teachers' unions actively trap high-needs minority students in failing schools, creating a conduit to the carceral state by denying them access to flexible, private alternatives. This perspective was shared by municipal leaders like Baltimore Mayor Kurt Schmoke, who argued that the public education monopoly, once a poor child's ticket out of the slums, had become an administrative trap. Despite court-ordered desegregation efforts, research by David Armor in Forced Justice showed that de facto racial segregation in public schools remained largely unchanged since 1968, indicating the limits of state-administered solutions.

The Intellectual Counter-Offensive and the Political Economy of Choice

The intellectual campaign to dismantle the state monopoly originated in the mid-20th century. Milton Friedman's 1955 essay, "The Role of Government in Education," argued that state-administered schools are natural monopolies that fail to benefit from market competition. Friedman proposed separating the public funding of education from its delivery, introducing vouchers that would follow the child to any approved private or public school. This free-market view contrasted sharply with Paul Samuelson’s public good theory, which classified education as an essential public resource that generates collective benefits, such as democratic cohesion, which cannot be captured by private transactions.

Libertarian thinkers developed diverse critiques of public schooling. Albert Jay Nock feared that a marketized educational system might still cater to a cheap and easy consumer preference, while Frank Chodorov maintained that parental decisions would always remain superior to state direction. Ayn Rand argued that public education serves as an instrument of state indoctrination, proposing tax credits as a practical transition toward complete privatization, whereas Murray Rothbard demanded a total, immediate separation of state and education.

These theoretical debates gained empirical support with John Chubb and Terry Moe's Politics, Markets, and America's Schools (1990), which argued that public schools are structurally incapable of reform because they are controlled by political coalitions rather than consumer demand. However, the political economy of school choice faced significant resistance from suburban homeowners. Studies by economists like Thomas Nechyba revealed that homes in high-performing public school districts command a substantial financial premium. Because vouchers lower the cost of private alternatives, they reduce this geographic premium, threatening to lower suburban property values while raising home values in low-income neighborhoods with poor public schools. Consequently, voting patterns on school choice initiatives revealed that homeowners without school-aged children voted to protect their property wealth: support for vouchers sat at only 39 percent among affluent suburbanites compared to 56 percent among residents in low-performing school zones.

To overcome this resistance, school choice advocates shifted their messaging. For decades, the voucher movement relied on a "values claim," championed by Catholic and evangelical families seeking to protect children from a secularized public curriculum. In the decade leading up to the Supreme Court's landmark Zelman v. Simmons-Harris (2002) decision, advocates replaced this with a "racial-justice claim," framing vouchers as a civil rights mechanism to liberate minority students from failing urban schools. This shift paved the way for the Court to uphold Cleveland’s voucher program. However, it also introduced a tension: the racial-justice claim relied on standardized testing and state accountability, which directly conflicted with the libertarian and religious demand for private school autonomy from government regulation.

Homeschooling as a Bottom-Up Private Association

Parallel to the policy campaign for school choice, the modern homeschooling movement emerged in the 1970s as a bottom-up, voluntary alternative to the public system. Influenced by Alexis de Tocqueville's observations of private associations, homeschooling grew organically without government aid or political vision. It emerged precisely as the federal government was expanding its educational bureaucracy, creating an ironic contrast between the bottom-up growth of home education and the top-down expansion of the federal Department of Education in 1979.

The early movement was defined by John Holt's progressive "unschooling" philosophy. Holt argued that formal schools are designed to produce compliant, subservient workers, and he urged parents to allow children to follow their natural curiosity. In 1977, he established Growing Without Schooling, creating the first national network for home educators. Raymond Moore provided a developmental complement to Holt's work, publishing Home Grown Kids in 1981. Moore argued that early institutionalized schooling was psychologically and morally detrimental, advocating instead for home-based instruction until age eight or nine.

In the 1980s, the movement experienced a demographic and ideological shift as evangelical Christians entered the sector. Driven by culture wars rhetoric and the popularity of James Dobson’s Focus on the Family broadcasts, these families viewed public schools as secular, state-sponsored institutions. This new wave took an antagonistic stance toward public school administrators, leading to intense legal conflicts over state compulsory attendance laws. To navigate this landscape, Michael Farris founded the Home School Legal Defense Association (HSLDA) in 1983. By the late 1980s and early 1990s, HSLDA led a successful state-by-state campaign to secure explicit legal protections, culminating in complete legalization across all 50 states by 1993.

This legal victory was accompanied by an internal "changing of the guard". Secular and moderate leaders like Raymond Moore were marginalized by a highly structured, conservative leadership coalition. New leaders, including Farris, Greg Harris, and Sue Welch, reframed homeschooling as a mechanism of social and political transformation. They envisioned training children to enter and reform the fields of government, education, and entertainment, introducing a structured social vision that contrasted with early child-led models. Public school defenders raised concerns about "socialization," which homeschool advocates dismissed as either a faux concern or a real anxiety that children were being educated outside the progressive ideological commitments of the educational establishment.

The Post-Pandemic Structural Shift in Home Education

The COVID-19 pandemic catalyzed a structural transformation in the homeschooling sector, shifting it from an alternative choice to one of the fastest-growing segments of American K-12 education. According to the Johns Hopkins Homeschool Research Lab, homeschooling grew at an average rate of 4. percent during the 2024–2025 academic year, nearly triple the pre-pandemic rate. Furthermore, 36 percent of reporting states recorded their highest homeschool enrollment numbers ever, exceeding even their pandemic-era peaks.

State-level data reveals significant geographic and micro-demographic variations. South Carolina recorded the nation's highest single-year growth rate at 21. percent, followed by Vermont (17 percent), Ohio (15 percent), and New Hampshire (14. percent). These trends fluctuated heavily between urban and rural school districts. In Georgia, overall growth was led by a 22 percent increase in Atlanta Public Schools, contrasted with a modest 4. percent increase in the suburban Cobb School District. Conversely, in Colorado, overall growth was driven by rural districts like Woodland Park and Poudre, while the urban Denver and Jeffco school districts experienced declines.

Socioeconomic and demographic indicators from 2019 and 2025 show a highly diverse homeschooling population. While non-Hispanic white families continue to homeschool at the highest overall rate (4. percent), the proportion of minority homeschoolers is rising rapidly, particularly among Hispanic students (1. percent rate in 2019). Geographically, homeschooling remains highly concentrated in rural areas (4. percent) compared to urban centers (2. percent) and suburbs (2. percent). Family structure remains a key predictor, with the highest rates occurring in two-parent households with a single income earner (6. percent).

This growth is supported by the emergence of hybrid educational models. Microschools, defined as small learning environments serving 5 to 15 students, have become a popular intermediary structure. A 2025 analysis by the National Microschooling Center revealed that over half of these institutions operate as parent-led homeschooling cooperatives, while 30 percent function as small private schools. On average, parents are willing to pay $433 per month to participate in these environments, and several states allow families to utilize public choice funds to cover these costs. Additionally, many states accommodate dual-enrollment, allowing homeschool students to enroll part-time in local public schools for specific courses, such as laboratory sciences, physical education, art, or foreign languages.

The Legislative Dismantling of the District Monopoly

The physical and administrative structures of the public school monopoly are facing unprecedented legislative challenges. As Cara Fitzpatrick observed in The Death of Public School, the conservative crusade to redefine education from a democratically governed public good to an individually determined private one has moved to the policy mainstream, driven by the slogan "fund students, not systems". This legislative push has resulted in 18 states enacting some form of universal private school choice, with at least 10 states operating universal Education Savings Account (ESA) programs.

The legislative sessions of 2024 and 2025 introduced major statutory expansions and structural modifications 35:

Arkansas (Senate Bill 625): Signed in April 2025, this legislation capped the proportion of Education Freedom Account funds that can be spent on extracurricular activities or transportation at 25 percent. It also reduced the program manager's administrative cost allowance from 5 percent to 2 percent and established a priority waitlist specifically for foster children. Crucially, it authorized program managers to bar families for "fraudulent conduct" without providing statutory definitions or examples, creating potential regulatory risks.

Oklahoma (Senate Bill 684): Enacted in May 2025, this bill modified the Parental Choice Tax Credit by authorizing human services agencies to share food stamp and Medicaid records with the state tax commission to verify low-income applicant status. It also altered the application window to run from March 15 to June 15 and required all participating private schools to submit verified student records to the tax commission by June 15.

South Carolina (Senate Bill 62): Signed in May 2025, this legislation repealed the 15,000-student participation cap on the state's Education Scholarship Trust Fund Program. It also raised the family income eligibility limit from 400 percent to 500 percent of the Federal Poverty Level for the 2026–2027 school year and added school uniforms as an eligible educational expense.

Utah (House Bill 455): Enacted in March 2025, this law imposed spending caps on extracurricular expenses, strictly limited payments for musical instruments, and restricted computer hardware purchases to once every three years. Most notably, it repealed a provision that adjusted the per-student ESA cap based on a five-year inflationary factor, limiting the program's ability to adjust to rising tuition costs.

Wyoming (House Bill 199): Signed in March 2025, this bill renamed the state's program the "Steamboat Legacy Scholarship Act" and expanded eligibility to all K-12 students, as well as pre-K students from families at or below 250 percent of the Federal Poverty Level. It raised the account cap from $6,000 to $7,000, allowed funds to be used for online courses, and established a priority waitlist that places siblings of current participants at the front of the line.

This legislative expansion has triggered significant consumer demand. A 2025 national survey revealed that 75 percent of U.S. parents—representing roughly 46 million families—actively considered, searched for, or enrolled at least one child in a new school, the highest level recorded in five years. After completing their search, parents were significantly less likely to choose a traditional neighborhood public school (27 percent) and highly likely to select private religious schools (21 percent), homeschooling (17 percent), or full-time online schools (15 percent). However, researchers note a potential survey response bias, as response rates for these parent satisfaction evaluations declined significantly to just 15 percent during the 2024–2025 cycle.

The GSU Position on the Single-Payer Paradox

GSU is a 501(c)(3) educational foundation. It accepts no state money. It collects no learner data. It requires no registration to use any resource it publishes. These choices were made deliberately, and the Single-Payer Paradox is the reason.

If the 2025 legislative wave continues — and the data suggests it will — a meaningful fraction of American private and homeschool education will, within a decade, be paid for by state Education Savings Accounts, Education Freedom Accounts, scholarship trust funds, and tax-credit mechanisms. Some of those programs will preserve the substantive independence they fund. Some will quietly attach the regulatory machinery that turned the 19th-century private and church school landscape into the consolidated public-school monopoly that took two hundred years to fracture.

The families who choose state-funded options should choose with their eyes open. The families who choose to fund their own children's education without state involvement should have somewhere serious to go for free, rigorous, multilingual curricular support that no legislature can quietly reshape. That second option is what GSU exists to be.

We are not arguing against school choice. We are arguing that the protection of independent education requires at least one free, government-money-free foundation working in parallel with whatever the state pays for. If the legislative wave delivers a generation of American children educated outside the public-school monopoly but inside a new state-funded curriculum monopoly, the freedom won in 2025 will be lost again by 2045. The Single-Payer Paradox is not a hypothesis. It is the historical record of how educational consolidation happens, applied to the next two decades.

"Vouchers can be a victory if they hold the state's hands off the substantive content of the schools they fund. They can also be a defeat if they bring the state's hands deep into the curriculum, the hiring, the values, and the philosophy of every private school and homeschool family that accepts the money. Which outcome we get is a policy choice — and a vigilance discipline — not an inevitability."

The educated population that protects its republic from re-monopolization is the population that has at least one place to learn what no one paid to teach them. GSU is built to be that place. Free. Unaccredited. Publicly verifiable. Donated. No state money. No registration. No conditions.

"We are not asking for permission. We are building the bridge."
