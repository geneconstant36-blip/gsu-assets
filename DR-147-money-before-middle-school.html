Money Before Middle School

What parents actually want schools to teach — and why the habits are set by age seven.

By Dr. Gene A. Constant  ·  Founding Chancellor  ·  June 19, 2026

We are raising a generation to inherit an economy they were never taught to navigate. The numbers are not subtle. In 2025, fifty-nine percent of American adults could not cover a one-thousand-dollar emergency from savings, and more than a quarter had no emergency savings at all.6 Bankrate's own analyst put it plainly: we have become a paycheck-to-paycheck nation. The question is no longer whether we should teach the young how money works. The question is why we keep waiting until it is nearly too late.

For decades, the standard answer was to teach personal finance in high school — a single semester, senior year, somewhere between the parking-lot privileges and the cap and gown. The trend is real and it is accelerating: by the Council for Economic Education's 2026 Survey of the States, thirty-nine states now require some personal finance coursework to graduate, twenty-six of them as a stand-alone class.2 That is progress worth applauding. But it answers the wrong question. It asks when can we squeeze this in before they leave us, when the science has been telling us for over a decade that the foundation is poured long before a child ever sees a high-school hallway.

The window closes at seven

In 2013, Cambridge behavioral researchers David Whitebread and Sue Bingham reviewed how children develop a relationship with money. Their report for the United Kingdom's Money Advice Service reached a conclusion that should have reorganized how we sequence economic education: the cognitive and metacognitive foundations of later financial behavior — the capacity to plan ahead, to exercise self-control, to delay a reward, to recognize that some choices cannot be undone — are largely in place by around the age of seven.1 By the time a formal finance course begins in a teenager's schedule, the underlying architecture is already built.

This does not mean a seven-year-old is a finished product, or that a person who missed the window is doomed. It means something more useful and more urgent: the most fertile soil for these habits is the early childhood years, and we have been planting in the wrong season. If we wait for adolescence to begin the serious work, we have already let the most formative decade of a child's development pass without a word about scarcity, value, or the simple, dignifying truth that money comes from work.

What parents actually want

Here is where the story turns hopeful, because parents are no longer silent and they are no longer split. In a 2025 survey for the American Bankers Association Foundation, eighty-seven percent of adults agreed that financial concepts should be taught in school — and when asked which subject most belongs in a core curriculum, they ranked economic and personal-finance education first, ahead of mathematics.4 A separate national poll found eighty-three percent want their state to require a dedicated course, and eighty-two percent of those who attended high school wish they had been required to take one.5 This demand crosses every line we are usually told divides us; the support holds steady whether the parent leans left, right, or neither.5

And parents are specific. They are not asking the early grades for monetary policy or the bond market. They are asking for applied, behavioral, everyday economics — the kind a child can practice at the kitchen table. When the research is sorted, the same six pillars surface again and again:

  • Needs versus wants, and scarcity. The first and most-requested lesson: resources are finite, every choice has a cost, and a want is not a need. This is the root of comparison shopping, of delayed gratification, of standing firm against a marketing machine built to erase the distinction.
  • Where money comes from. That money is earned through work and skill — not dispensed by a machine in a wall or summoned by a plastic card. Effort creates value, and different skills are valued differently.
  • Saving, budgeting, and banking. Dividing money into spend, save, and share; building and keeping a simple budget; and the quiet miracle of interest — money that earns money.
  • Borrowing, credit, and debt. The plain rule first — borrowed money must be paid back, usually with a penalty — then, for older children, the difference between debt that builds and debt that buries.
  • Entrepreneurship. Not as a path to a corporation, but as a vehicle for grit and creativity: spot a need, count your costs, set a price, earn a profit, and survive a failure in a place where failure is safe.
  • Taxes and the common good. The gap between what you earn and what you take home, and the civic reason for it — that pooled money builds the roads, the schools, and the fire trucks none of us could buy alone.

The paradox at the kitchen table

If parents want this so badly, why don't they simply teach it themselves? Many try, and many of the best lessons a child ever learns about money come from watching a parent live them. But the data exposes an honest paradox. Parents feel confident discussing basic saving, yet that confidence falls sharply when the subject turns to structured budgeting, and falls further still at credit and debt avoidance — the very topics that wreck adult lives. Roughly a third of parents admit they avoid talking with their children about the family's real financial situation at all.8 The old "money silence" has not vanished; it has simply moved into the rooms where the hardest lessons live.

This is not a failure of love. It is a failure of equipping. And when we leave economic education to whatever a household happens to know, we do not get a level field — we get the opposite. Financial-literacy gaps open early and track race, income, and parental education, and they open along gender lines before a girl is old enough to know she is being underestimated.9 A model that relies entirely on at-home transmission quietly hands the children of capable households a head start and tells everyone else to figure it out later. That is how knowledge gaps harden into wealth gaps, one generation at a time.

The cure is not a lecture

The fix is not to hand a seven-year-old a textbook earlier. Whitebread's own warning was that simply imparting information does not work at this age.1 The cure is experience. Children learn money the way they learn everything that lasts — by doing it, getting it wrong, and doing it again. The most effective elementary programs are built on exactly this principle: classroom economies where students earn a paycheck, pay a rent, and manage a small budget; simulated marketplaces where they price and sell; demonstrations that make scarcity something you feel in your hands rather than something you hear in a definition.10

None of it requires a finance degree. It requires three jars on a counter. It requires letting a child hold five dollars in a store and own the trade-off. It requires a real lemonade stand with real costs and a real, hard-won profit. The economy is not an abstraction we need to import into a child's life — it is already running in every grocery line, every gas pump, every chore. The work is simply to name it out loud, early, and often.
