GSU Trades Dictionary

The everyday language of the skilled trades — the words that show up in electrical, plumbing, repair, and safety work — explained in plain English, free, for anyone curious about a hands-on career. These are foundations anyone can learn; the full how-to lives in the trade manuals and in GENO. Safety terms are marked with why they matter, because in the trades, knowing the word can keep you alive.

CircuitA complete loop that lets electric current flow. Break the loop anywhere and the current stops.
Voltage (volts)The electrical "pressure" that pushes current through a circuit.
Amperage (amps)The amount of electric current actually flowing. Amps do the work — and amps are what can injure a person.
WattA measure of electrical power, equal to volts multiplied by amps. It tells you how much energy a device uses.
Circuit breakerA safety switch that automatically cuts power when a circuit draws too much current.Why it matters: It exists to stop wires from overheating and starting a fire — never defeat or oversize one.
GFCIA Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter — an outlet or breaker that shuts power off in a fraction of a second if it senses electricity leaking, such as toward water or a person.Why it matters: Required near sinks, baths, and outdoors; it is a life-saver, not a luxury.
GroundingA deliberate safe path that carries stray electricity into the earth instead of through your body.Why it matters: It is one of the most important safety systems in any building.
TorqueA turning or twisting force. Bolts are tightened to a specific torque, not "as hard as you can."Why it matters: Too loose fails; too tight snaps or strips — the spec exists for a reason.
FastenerAny device that holds parts together: a screw, bolt, nail, or rivet.
Shut-off valveA valve that stops the flow of water or gas to a fixture — or to the whole home.Why it matters: Knowing where yours is before an emergency can save your house from flooding or worse.
PSIPounds per square inch — a measure of pressure, used for tires, water systems, and air tools.
MultimeterA handheld tool that measures voltage, current, and resistance.Why it matters: It is the basic diagnostic instrument that lets you test instead of guess.
Load-bearingA wall or beam that carries weight from the structure above it.Why it matters: Removing one without proper temporary support can collapse what's overhead — always verify before cutting into a wall.
Lockout / tagoutA safety practice: shut off power to equipment, lock it off, and tag it so no one can switch it on while you work.
PPEPersonal Protective Equipment — the gear that protects you: safety glasses, gloves, hearing protection, a respirator.
CombustionBurning — a reaction of fuel, oxygen, and ignition. It runs furnaces, water heaters, and engines.Why it matters: When combustion is incomplete, it can produce deadly carbon monoxide.
Carbon monoxide (CO)A colorless, odorless, poisonous gas produced by incomplete burning of fuel.Why it matters: You cannot see or smell it — which is exactly why working CO detectors save lives.
SolderingJoining metal pieces by melting a filler metal into the joint; flux is the paste that cleans the metal so the bond holds.
Water hammerThe banging sound in pipes when fast-moving water is stopped suddenly.Why it matters: It is a warning sign of pressure stress that can loosen joints over time.
Building codeThe legal minimum safety standards for construction and repair in your area.Why it matters: Passing inspection means your work meets code — the baseline that keeps people safe.
