Why Bigger Is Not Better

The rookie instinct is to oversize "just to be safe." An oversized air conditioner short-cycles: it slams the temperature down, shuts off before it has wrung the humidity out of the air, and leaves the room cold, clammy, and hard on the compressor. Correct sizing beats big sizing every time.

The rule of thumb here — about 20 BTU per square foot, adjusted for the room's real conditions — is how a tech ballparks a window unit or single-zone mini-split. Whole-home equipment deserves the real instrument: an ACCA Manual J load calculation, which accounts for windows, orientation, infiltration, and duct losses room by room.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many BTUs per square foot?

Roughly 20 BTU per square foot for cooling, adjusted up for high ceilings, sun, poor insulation, occupants, and kitchen heat, and down for shade and tight construction. Manual J is the accurate standard.

What happens if I oversize?

Short-cycling: fast cooling, poor dehumidification, a cold-and-clammy room, and premature compressor wear. Size to the load, not to fear.

What is a "ton" of cooling?

12,000 BTU per hour — the heat absorbed by melting one ton of ice in 24 hours. A 24,000 BTU mini-split is a 2-ton unit.

Is this a substitute for Manual J?

No. It is a free educational estimator for room units and single zones. Whole-home systems should be sized by a qualified HVAC professional using ACCA Manual J.
