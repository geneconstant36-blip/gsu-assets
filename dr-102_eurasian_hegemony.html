The Eurasian Hegemony

A counterfactual analysis of the 20th century without United States intervention — and what it reveals about the actual structure of the world we inherited

Dr. Gene A. Constant, DBA· ~5,700 words· 23 min read

Modern historical methodology and geopolitical analysis increasingly employ counterfactual thought experimentation to test the structural integrity of historical turning points. By systematically altering a single massive variable — the existence and global projection of the United States — this article conducts a rigorous stress test on the foundations of the modern world order, traversing the redirected European diaspora, the Great War without American intervention, the Lend-Lease counterfactual, and what a permanent Eurasian totalitarian division would have meant for human rights, technology, and religious freedom.

The Architecture of Historical Counterfactuals

Classical historians sometimes view history as a continuous, deterministic process in which all preceding events are strictly necessary to bring about a specific, immutable outcome. However, modern historical methodology increasingly employs counterfactual thought experimentation to test the structural integrity of historical turning points and to challenge prognostic reflexes. By systematically altering a single, albeit massive, variable — the existence and subsequent global projection of the United States of America — it becomes possible to conduct a rigorous stress test on the foundations of the modern world order. In counterfactual analysis, the objective is to trace the minimal rewrites of history and observe their ripple effects across political, economic, demographic, and sociological spectrums over a prolonged temporal horizon.

When examining a hypothetical world where the United States never existed as a geopolitical entity, a sanctuary for the displaced, or an economic powerhouse, the analysis must traverse several critical, interconnected epochs. The absence of the American republic fundamentally rewrites the demographic migrations of the nineteenth century, the military and economic outcomes of the First World War, and the ultimate climax of the Second World War. Without the intervention of American military logistics, democratic philosophy, and financial capital, the mid-twentieth century descends into an unmitigated struggle between the totalitarian titans of Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union, eventually stabilizing into a horrific, permanent division of the Eurasian landmass.

The Nineteenth Century Diaspora: Redirected Demographics and the Loss of Sanctuary

Between 1846 and 1939, an unprecedented demographic shift occurred, with an estimated 55 to 60 million people emigrating from Europe to overseas destinations. The United States absorbed the overwhelming majority of this human capital, acting as the primary destination for over 32 million immigrants between 1821 and 1932. In a world devoid of the American sanctuary, this massive wave of human migration would not simply evaporate — it would be violently redirected to alternative destinations such as Argentina, Canada, Brazil, and Australia.

The implications of this demographic diversion are profound. The United States was uniquely defined by a constitution that guaranteed that tradition, ethnic inheritance, and territory could never be fully naturalized as the sole basis for nationality. In stark contrast, European forms of nationalism were deeply rooted in exclusionary blood-and-soil ideologies. Without the United States providing a vast, conceptually virgin soil for religious and ethnic communities to implant themselves autonomously, religious minorities and dissenting Protestant denominations would have faced dire prospects.

European dissidents fleeing state-sponsored religious persecution would have overwhelmed the infrastructural and assimilative capacities of nations like Argentina and Brazil. By 1914, 58 percent of Argentina's eight million inhabitants were already first- or second-generation immigrants. Tripling or quadrupling that influx with culturally disparate Eastern and Northern European refugees would likely have fractured these nascent states. The very concept of religious freedom as a constitutionally protected right, separated entirely from state apparatus, would have lacked its most powerful global champion.

The Great War and the European Economic Abyss

The geopolitical stabilization of the twentieth century was inextricably linked to American financial capital and military intervention. During the First World War, the United States walked a tightrope of neutrality for three years. Without the injection of the American Expeditionary Forces, which brought fresh millions into the theater of war and fundamentally turned the tide in favor of Britain and France in 1918, the Great War would likely have ended in a brutal, exhausted stalemate or a pyrrhic victory for the Central Powers.

More critically, the absence of the United States would have removed the financial engine that prevented total European economic collapse in the 1920s. The First World War radically altered the global economy — by 1920, the United States had transitioned from the world's largest debtor to its absolute largest creditor. The U.S.-sponsored Dawes Plan of 1924 lent millions of dollars from New York banks directly to the Weimar Republic, allowing Germany to modernize its heavy industry, stabilize its currency, and pay reparations to France and Britain, who in turn used those funds to stabilize their own war-torn economies.

In an alternate timeline lacking American economic infrastructure, this fragile triad of international finance evaporates completely. Without the Dawes Plan, the Weimar Republic's economy would have completely disintegrated by the mid-1920s. France and Britain, deprived of reparation payments, would have faced sovereign defaults. The mass unemployment and hyperinflation that fueled the rise of extremist political parties — both fascist and communist — would have accelerated exponentially. The global economic collapse, untethered from the specific trigger of the 1929 Wall Street crash, would have occurred much earlier, plunging a still-bleeding Europe into absolute radicalism. The parliamentary democracies of Western Europe, lacking the financial backstop of a rising superpower, would have rapidly succumbed to totalitarian forces promising order through authoritarian control and command economies.

The Totalitarian Crucible: World War II and the Fall of the Allies

The culmination of European economic collapse and unchecked political radicalism leads directly to a Second World War dominated entirely by Eurasian despots. In a world without the United States, the strategic viability of both Great Britain and the Soviet Union against the Nazi war machine approaches zero.

The Strangulation of Great Britain

Following the armistice with France, Hitler's Supreme Command planned Operation Sea Lion, the amphibious invasion of the United Kingdom. According to Hitler's Directive No. 16, the invasion force would have landed on a wide front from Ramsgate to the Isle of Wight, utilizing upwards of 40 divisions. While the historical execution of Sea Lion was canceled due to the Luftwaffe's failure to achieve air superiority, the long-term survival of Britain was highly dependent on American logistical support. Without the Lend-Lease Act or the Destroyers for Bases agreement, the Royal Navy would have been dangerously overextended. Deprived of critical maritime reinforcements, aviation fuel, and munitions, the British Isles would have been starved into submission through a protracted, unbreakable naval blockade by German U-boats.

The Collapse of the Eastern Front

The survival of the Soviet Union on the Eastern Front was intrinsically tied to American material aid. Between October 1941 and July 1945, American Lend-Lease transferred an astonishing $11 billion in goods to the USSR — equivalent to over $250 billion in modern currency. This massive influx included millions of tons of foodstuffs, raw materials (such as aluminum, copper, manganese, and coal), advanced industrial equipment, and vital transport vehicles. Without these supplies, the Red Army's offensive capability collapses by mid-1942, and the strategic depth that allowed the Soviet Union to absorb and eventually reverse the Wehrmacht's drive simply does not exist.

The Permanent Division: A Eurasia Without America

Without American intervention, the Second World War does not produce a clean Allied victory. It produces an exhausted standoff between two totalitarian powers — Nazi Germany controlling most of Europe west of the Urals, and Imperial Japan dominant across East Asia and the Pacific Rim. The Soviet Union, having collapsed under combined German military pressure and the absence of Lend-Lease, fragments into Nazi-controlled territories in the west and Japanese-controlled or independent successor states in Siberia.

The mid-twentieth-century world that emerges is fundamentally different from the one we inherited. Generalplan Ost — the Nazi plan for the demographic re-engineering of Eastern Europe through mass deportation, enslavement, and extermination of Slavic populations — proceeds without external interruption. The Holocaust, far from being interrupted by Allied victory in 1945, accelerates and expands across the entirety of the European Jewish diaspora and the Slavic, Roma, and dissident populations of the conquered East. The total death toll over the following two decades plausibly exceeds 100 million.

The Long Shadow of Permanent Totalitarianism

Without American power as a counterweight, the reconstruction of liberal democracy in Western Europe never occurs. The Marshall Plan does not exist. Western European parliamentary democracies, having collapsed during the war, are not rebuilt. The European Union as we know it cannot form because there is no democratic substrate to unite. NATO does not exist. The post-1945 wave of decolonization proceeds under entirely different terms — colonized peoples seeking independence find themselves choosing between two totalitarian patrons rather than between the Western liberal model and Soviet communism.

The ideological and material conditions that produced the late twentieth century — the welfare state, mass higher education, the civil rights revolution, the women's movement, the technology boom — emerge in radically diminished forms or do not emerge at all. The intellectual and scientific diaspora that fled Europe for the United States in the 1930s and 1940s (Einstein, von Braun, von Neumann, Fermi, Tesla, hundreds more) has nowhere comparable to flee to. The atomic bomb, the transistor, the computer, the internet — innovations heavily dependent on this American sanctuary for the European intellectual class — proceed on radically slower trajectories or in malign forms under totalitarian control.

What the Counterfactual Reveals About the World We Inherited

Counterfactual analysis is not historical fantasy. Done rigorously, it is a stress test that reveals the structural significance of variables that day-to-day history tends to take for granted. The counterfactual examined here yields several findings about the actual world.

First, American power in the twentieth century was not optional. It was load-bearing. Remove it, and the structures that depended on it — democratic Western Europe, the global financial system, the postwar liberal order, the religious-freedom precedent, the technological renaissance — collapse or never form. Second, the costs of American hegemony, real as they are, must be measured against the alternative that nothing else was available to play the same structural role. Third, the survival of free societies in the twentieth century was contingent rather than inevitable. The forces of totalitarianism were genuinely capable of winning. They lost not because they were structurally weaker but because a specific external power chose, repeatedly, to intervene against them at critical moments.

The fourth finding is the most consequential for present-day civic engagement. The structures the twentieth-century American intervention created are themselves contingent. They were built. They can be unbuilt. Anyone who takes the postwar order for granted has not done the counterfactual seriously. Free societies survive because someone, somewhere, keeps choosing to build them. The lesson of this counterfactual is not American triumphalism but civic responsibility: the inheritance is real, the inheritance is fragile, and the inheritance does not maintain itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is counterfactual history, and is it a legitimate methodology? +

Counterfactual history is a methodology that systematically alters one or more historical variables to trace the ripple effects on subsequent events. It is increasingly accepted in academic historical methodology as a legitimate analytical tool, particularly for testing the structural significance of historical turning points. The methodology has limitations — distant counterfactuals become increasingly speculative — but proximate counterfactuals (what happens if X variable is removed at Y moment) can yield rigorous analytical insights. The article uses counterfactual analysis to stress-test the structural significance of American power in the twentieth century.

What was the Dawes Plan, and why does the article emphasize it? +

The Dawes Plan of 1924 was a U.S.-sponsored financial restructuring that lent millions of dollars from New York banks to the Weimar Republic, allowing Germany to modernize its heavy industry, stabilize its currency, and pay reparations to France and Britain. The article emphasizes it because it represents a concrete, identifiable American intervention that prevented European economic collapse in the 1920s. Without this intervention, the article argues, Weimar Germany disintegrates by the mid-1920s and the cascade of consequences arrives years earlier and far more severely.

What was Generalplan Ost? +

Generalplan Ost (General Plan East) was the Nazi plan for the demographic re-engineering of Eastern Europe following German victory in the East. It involved the mass deportation, enslavement, and extermination of Slavic, Jewish, Roma, and other populations — calculated by the historians who have studied surviving documents to involve the planned death of tens of millions of people. In the actual historical timeline, Generalplan Ost was interrupted by Allied victory in 1945. In the counterfactual examined by this article, it proceeds to completion.

Doesn't this analysis amount to American triumphalism? +

The article addresses this directly. The counterfactual is not a celebration of American power in the abstract. It is an analytical exercise that reveals how load-bearing American intervention was at specific historical moments. The article's concluding finding is that this places a civic burden on Americans (and on the inheritors of the postwar order more broadly), not a triumphalist license. The structures that depend on American power are contingent; they can be unbuilt; the responsibility of maintaining them belongs to whoever currently inherits them.
