The Tyranny of Debt

How borrowing transitioned from a tool of financial flexibility into a system of systemic discipline — and the strategies through which the free citizen reclaims agency

Dr. Gene A. Constant, DBA· ~3,700 words· 15 min read

The contemporary global order is increasingly defined not by the exchange of goods or the production of value, but by the management of obligations. While classical economic theory posits debt as a neutral, temporary bridge between present needs and future income, a deeper political-economic analysis suggests that debt has evolved into a sophisticated technology of power. This article examines how borrowing transitioned from a tool of financial flexibility into a system of systemic discipline — and the philosophical and practical strategies through which the free citizen might reclaim agency.

The Historical Genesis of the Capitalized State

The modern architecture of debt-based power finds its structural origins in the late seventeenth century, specifically with the founding of the Bank of England in 1694. This period marked a fundamental shift from the personal debt of a monarch — which was frequently subject to repudiation — to a permanent, institutionalized national debt. This transformation allowed the English state to fund perpetual war-making by capitalizing future tax revenues, effectively creating a capitalized state whose very survival was linked to the satisfaction of private creditors.

The 1694 Paradigm and the Monetization of Warfare

Prior to the 1694 paradigm, credit was often localized and personal. The innovation of the Bank of England was the production of commercial money as interest-bearing debt. This meant that the circulating medium of the economy was no longer a commodity with intrinsic value but a promissory note representing a claim on future labor and resources. By allowing the state to issue bonds that were essentially perpetual, the government secured a mechanism for continuous military expansion while providing the emerging financial elite with a normal rate of return secured by the coercive power of the state. The state's ability to wage war became contingent upon its creditworthiness, leading to a self-reinforcing cycle where debt funded the conquest of territories, which in turn provided the resources to service the expanding debt. This debt-war nexus established a new hierarchy of power where the creditor class held ultimate leverage over state policy.

Colonial Expansion as Debt Intensification

As European powers expanded globally, the technology of debt served as a primary instrument of colonial control. Colonial administrators utilized accounting and monetization as weapons to integrate indigenous societies into the imperial market. By banning alternative currencies and imposing taxes payable only in colonial debt-money, imperial powers forced local populations into the production of cash crops or the extraction of minerals to acquire the currency needed to settle their obligations. The Royal African Company (RAC), established in 1660, serves as a quintessential example: investors were not merely funding a trade venture, they were capitalizing the company's capacity for horrific exploitation, with their returns contingent upon the efficiency of human bondage. This historical precedent illustrates that debt under capitalism is not merely a financial arrangement but a technology for capitalizing the exploitative capacity of entire regions and populations.

The Mathematical Trap: Compound Interest and the Growth Mandate

At the core of the debt-as-power thesis lies the mathematical reality of interest-bearing credit. When the money supply is created as debt, the total amount owed — principal plus interest — always exceeds the total amount of money in circulation. This creates a systemic requirement for exponential growth, as new debt must be constantly issued to provide the liquidity necessary to pay off the interest on existing obligations. To prevent mass default, the real economy must grow at a rate that keeps pace with these financial claims. However, because the planet's biological and mineral resources are finite, the requirement for infinite growth leads to the annihilation of the human and natural substance of society.

In the framework of Capital as Power, debt functions as a form of strategic sabotage — power exerted not through pure productivity but through the ability to limit and direct the flow of social energy for distributional ends. By mortgaging the future, creditors ensure that the trajectory of human development remains tethered to the requirement of debt service, effectively foreclosing on the realization of alternative social arrangements.

The Biopolitics of Indebtedness

Debt is not merely an external economic burden; it is a biopolitical technique that reshapes human subjectivity. Drawing on the philosophical insights of Friedrich Nietzsche and contemporary theorists like Maurizio Lazzarato, debt can be understood as a primary mechanism for the production and control of people.

The Interiorization of Guilt and the Morality of Debt

Nietzsche famously observed the linguistic and psychological link between debt (Schulden) and guilt (Schuld). In the modern era, this moral framing is used to isolate debtors and prevent collective resistance. To default on a debt is framed not as a financial outcome of an asymmetrical risk, but as a blasphemy, shame, and dishonor that reflects a failure of personal character. This moralization serves several functions:

  • Victim-Blaming. During crises such as the subprime mortgage collapse, the narrative focused on irresponsible borrowers rather than the structural predatory practices of the banking sector.
  • Disciplinary Surveillance. Credit scores act as a vicious cycle of surveillance, where a person's ability to secure employment or housing is contingent upon their history of servility to creditors.
  • Self-Regulation. Debt forces individuals to become entrepreneurs of the self, where they freely choose to take on debt for education or survival, only to find themselves tethered to a lifetime of heteronomous labor.

The Tyranny of the Debt Trap: India and Thailand

The most extreme manifestations of debt as a technology of power are found in the debt traps of the Global South. In India, neoliberal reforms in the 1990s led to the withdrawal of state subsidies for agriculture, forcing farmers to turn to usurious local moneylenders and microfinance institutions. Between 1995 and 2010, an epidemic of over 250,000 farmer suicides was recorded, with personal indebtedness cited as the common driver. Similarly, in Thailand, debt is a primary mechanism for human trafficking. Families struggling to repay mortgages may use their daughters as collateral for loans. These girls are then trafficked into sex industries, where exorbitant interest ensures that their labor can never fully repay the principal — creating a state of permanent debt bondage. In these cases, debt serves as the essential glue that holds together systems of modern slavery and exploitation.

Sovereign Tyranny and the Geopolitics of Austerity

The power of debt is equally potent at the level of the nation-state. International financial institutions like the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank utilize sovereign debt as a lever to impose austerity and neoliberal restructuring on vulnerable countries. When a nation faces a balance-of-payments crisis, it often turns to the IMF as a lender of last resort. However, this assistance is contingent upon strict conditions known as structural adjustment. These typically include: cuts to public services (reductions in spending on infrastructure, education, and healthcare to prioritize debt service to foreign bondholders); privatization (the sale of state-owned enterprises to private investors, often at fire-sale prices); and monetary discipline (the adoption of high interest rates and inflation targeting that benefits creditors while suppressing domestic wages and demand).

The result is a tiered global hierarchy in which sovereign nations of the Global South operate under terms set by creditor institutions headquartered in the wealthy North. The formal label is loan conditionality. The functional reality is the partial outsourcing of national economic policy to entities that are not democratically accountable to the populations whose lives the policy will reshape. The disciplinary logic of austerity is not an unintended consequence of debt service. It is the mechanism.

The Resistance of the Free Citizen

Recognizing debt as a technology of power is the first move toward resisting it. The second is recognizing that the resistance is partly individual (refusing to take on unnecessary debt, refusing to internalize debtor guilt) and partly structural (refusing to accept the framing that debt service is a moral obligation that overrides all other social claims). The free citizen, in this analysis, is the person who has examined how debt operates and chosen to organize their financial life around minimum dependency on creditor institutions. Not zero dependency — that is rarely achievable — but the minimum compatible with a real life. Every dollar of unnecessary debt voluntarily declined is a small refusal of the creditocracy. Every household budget oriented around capability rather than consumption is a small reclamation of agency.

It is debt-literacy as practical sovereignty — a free citizen's working knowledge of the technology that shapes their economic life, and the practical decisions that follow from that knowledge.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does 'debt as power' mean? +

Debt as power is the analytical framework, developed within Capital as Power theory and related political-economic literature, that views debt not as a neutral financial instrument but as a technology used to discipline populations, extract wealth across generations, and constrain sovereign autonomy. The framework treats the institutional architecture of credit (central banks, bond markets, IMF conditionality, credit scoring) as one of the primary mechanisms through which power is exercised in modern capitalist economies.

Why does the article focus on the year 1694? +

1694 is the founding year of the Bank of England, which the article identifies as the structural origin of modern debt-based power. The Bank of England's innovation was the production of commercial money as interest-bearing debt, transforming sovereign borrowing from personal monarchical debt (often repudiated) into a permanent institutionalized national debt that bound the state to private creditors and funded continuous warfare and colonial expansion.

What is the 'creditocracy'? +

Creditocracy is the term for a political-economic order in which the creditor class holds ultimate leverage over state policy, household economic life, and the trajectory of social development. It describes a condition where political legitimacy increasingly derives not from democratic consent but from the maintenance of creditworthiness and the satisfaction of bond-market expectations. The term has been developed by various political economists and is used in the article to characterize the contemporary global order.

Is the article anti-debt as a moral position? +

No. The article is a structural analysis of debt as a technology of power, not a moral condemnation of borrowing as such. The closing argument frames the practical response as debt-literacy rather than ideological anti-debt — the working knowledge of how credit operates, the practical decisions that follow from that knowledge, and the orientation toward minimum dependency on creditor institutions compatible with a real life. Some debt is unavoidable. Most consumer debt is unnecessary, and the difference matters.

What is structural adjustment? +

Structural adjustment refers to the conditions imposed by international financial institutions like the IMF and World Bank when they extend lending to countries facing balance-of-payments crises. These conditions typically include cuts to public services, privatization of state-owned enterprises, monetary discipline through high interest rates, and currency devaluation. The disciplinary logic of austerity is not an unintended consequence of debt service — it is the mechanism through which sovereign autonomy is constrained in favor of creditor interests.
