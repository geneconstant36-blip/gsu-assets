The Neighborhood

The Sun Has a Family

Our Sun is not alone. Around him circle eight planets, dozens of moons, thousands of asteroids, and a distant cloud of comets. They are held together by his gravity like a father keeping his children close. All of them together are called our Solar System. Every child on Earth lives inside it.

The Solar System is the first subtopic of the K-5 Wonder Stage because it is the one every child asks about first. It is the one you can see with your own eyes on any clear night. The Moon is there overhead. Venus and Jupiter are the brightest points of light nearby. A telescope or a pair of binoculars turns the neighborhood into a place worth visiting.

This hub gives a curious child ages 5 to 11 the whole neighborhood in fourteen short chapters. The first book in the K-5 Wonder Series, The Sun’s Family, sits at the center of it. Around the book are twelve kitchen-table experiments, thirty vocabulary words in a friendly dictionary, and a teach-then-play game that earns a wall card and eventually a Solar System cert strip. The Framework

Four Rooms, One Family

A young sovereign can hold the whole Solar System in her head by remembering four rooms. Start with the Sun in the middle. Work outward. That is how astronomers organize it too.

1
The Sun
A giant ball of hydrogen turning itself into helium and light. The reason every other room exists. Weighs as much as a million Earths piled together.
2
The Inner Planets
Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. Small, rocky, and close to the fire. Two of them have moons. One of them has us. All four could fit inside Jupiter with room to spare.
3
The Outer Planets
Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. Huge, gassy or icy, and far from the fire. Every one of them has rings. Every one of them has moons — Jupiter alone has ninety-five.
4
The Icy Edge
Beyond Neptune lies the Kuiper Belt (where Pluto lives), and beyond that the Oort Cloud where comets are born. The edge of the neighborhood, where the Sun is just another bright star.

Do It Today

Twelve Kitchen Table Experiments

Every experiment uses items already in the house. Every one takes ten to twenty minutes. Every one lets a child prove something about the neighborhood with her own hands. Full instructions land in Chapter 13 of The Sun’s Family.

1. The Fruit Solar SystemA scale model using a grapefruit, a peppercorn, and things in between. The distances will surprise you.
2. The Sunbeam on Your PalmA piece of the Sun that just traveled 93 million miles — feel it now.
3. Watching Venus at DuskThe brightest star in the sky near sunset is not a star at all. Find it three nights in a row.
4. Day and Night with a FlashlightShow a younger sibling why we have day and night. Ball plus flashlight plus dark room.
5. The Moon Phase JournalSketch the Moon on paper every night for four weeks. Watch the pattern emerge.
6. Orange and Flashlight EclipseRecreate a solar eclipse and a lunar eclipse on your kitchen counter.
7. The Rusty Plate of MarsA pinch of paprika under a magnifying glass. That is the color of Mars.
8. Jupiter’s Moons Through BinocularsOn a clear night, find Jupiter. Look for four bright dots in a line beside it. Galileo saw exactly this in 1610.
9. Saturn’s Rings in a JarPebbles, ice cubes, and imagination. What the rings would look like up close.
10. The Sideways SpinSpin a top upright. Then push it over. That is how Uranus spins.
11. The Comet SlushMix dirt into a cup of ice slush. Congratulations, you have made a comet.
12. The Backyard SundialA stick, a paper plate, and a sunny day. The oldest clock in the world.

Straight Answers

Frequently Asked Questions

How many planets are in our Solar System?
Eight. Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Pluto was called the ninth planet from 1930 until 2006, when scientists reclassified it as a dwarf planet after discovering that many other Pluto-sized objects existed in the Kuiper Belt. Pluto did not change — our definition of "planet" did. The Sun’s Family explains why in a chapter a nine-year-old will find satisfying.
What is the difference between a star and a planet?
A star makes its own light by fusing hydrogen into helium at its core — the Sun is our closest example. A planet does not make its own light; it shines because it reflects the light of a nearby star. Every point of light in the night sky is either a star far away or a planet in our own Solar System. Practically the only way to tell the difference by eye is that stars twinkle and planets do not.
Why does the Moon change shape?
The Moon does not actually change shape — it just gets lit up differently as it orbits Earth. Sunlight always hits half the Moon. When we see a full moon, we are looking at the whole lit half. When we see a crescent, we are looking at the Moon almost sideways so only a sliver of the lit half faces us. The whole cycle takes about 29 days. Chapter 6 of The Sun’s Family covers it with a flashlight and an orange.
How far away is the Sun?
The Sun is about 93 million miles from Earth. That is far enough that sunlight, moving at the fastest speed in the universe, takes about 8 minutes and 20 seconds to reach us. Meaning every sunbeam you feel on your skin left the Sun before you sat down. Jupiter is 5 times farther, and Neptune is 30 times farther — sunlight takes over 4 hours to reach Neptune.
Is anything alive on any other planet?
Scientists have not found life anywhere else in our Solar System yet — but they are still looking. The most interesting places to look are Mars (which had liquid water in the ancient past), Europa (a moon of Jupiter with an ocean under an icy crust), and Enceladus (a moon of Saturn that shoots water into space through cracks in its ice). Nothing is confirmed. But if it is out there, we are very close to being able to find it.
What age is this content written for?
The K-5 Wonder Series is written for children ages 5 to 11. A five-year-old will do best with a parent reading a chapter aloud. A nine-year-old can read on her own and start doing the kitchen-table experiments without help. The book is not baby talk — it uses real vocabulary and defines any hard word the first time it appears.
